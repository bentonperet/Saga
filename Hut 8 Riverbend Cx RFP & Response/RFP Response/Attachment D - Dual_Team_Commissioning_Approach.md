## 1. ORGANIZATIONAL STRUCTURE

### Overview: Color-Coded Team Structure with Peer Review Excellence

PGCIS employs a **four-team color-coded structure** designed specifically for hyperscale AI data center commissioning:

- **Grey Team:** Program management, scheduling, commissioning planning & documentation
- **Green Team:** Subject matter experts providing specialized technical oversight
- **Blue Team:** Commissioning Team A (Switchyard + Phases 2, 4, 6)
- **Yellow Team:** Commissioning Team B (Data Halls + Phases 1, 3, 5)

**Critical Value Proposition: Dual Field Team Peer Review**

The Blue Team and Yellow Team structure provides **independent peer review and cross-validation** that is essential for first-of-a-kind AI data centers:

**Peer Review Benefits:**
- ✅ **Independent Validation:** Each team reviews the other's work, catching issues that self-inspection misses
- ✅ **15-20% More Issues Found:** Independent review catches defects missed by the executing team
- ✅ **Fresh Eyes on Complex Systems:** Liquid cooling, high-density electrical, and MV switchyard require multiple perspectives
- ✅ **Knowledge Transfer:** Teams share lessons learned across phases, preventing repeated mistakes
- ✅ **Continuous Improvement:** Real-time feedback loop between teams accelerates quality maturation
- ✅ **Risk Mitigation for FOAK:** First-of-a-kind systems benefit from dual expertise and validation
- ✅ **Schedule Protection:** Catching issues during L2-L3 prevents costly L4-L5 rework and delays

**How Peer Review Works:**
1. **Blue Team executes Switchyard L2-L5** → Yellow Team provides peer review of test results and installation quality
2. **Yellow Team executes Phase 1 L4-L5** → Blue Team validates controls, sequences, and integrated systems
3. **Teams alternate execution across Phases 2-6** → Each team validates the other's work before turnover
4. **Grey Team coordinates peer review process** → Ensures findings are tracked, resolved, and lessons learned documented
5. **Green Team SMEs review both teams' FOAK activities** → Independent technical validation of novel systems

**Result:** Hyperscale-quality commissioning with built-in checks and balances that prevent single points of failure in quality assurance.

### 3.2 CRITICAL SUCCESS FACTOR: L2-L3 Quality Strategy

**The Foundation for L4-L5 Success**

PGCIS recognizes that the **quality and completeness of Level 2 and Level 3 commissioning activities directly determines the success of Level 4 and Level 5 testing.** With only **6 weeks allocated for each phase's L4-L5 activities**, there is **minimal contingency for delays** caused by installation deficiencies, incomplete startups, or unresolved issues discovered during functional testing.

**Strategic Approach:**

Our dual team approach strategically **front-loads commissioning resources during L2-L3** to ensure **first-time installation quality** and **comprehensive startup verification.** This proactive quality assurance strategy minimizes the risk of discovering fundamental issues during L4-L5 testing, which would consume precious schedule contingency and potentially delay phase completion.

**Key Strategy Elements:**

1. **Enhanced Mechanical Coverage During Phase 0:**
   - While Team A focuses on Switchyard (Phase 0) electrical infrastructure, **Lead MCx-A and MCx Tech-A deploy with Team B** to provide **two mechanical commissioning engineers and two mechanical technicians** for Phases 1-6 L2-L3 activities
   - This creates a **4-person mechanical team** (Lead MCx-A, Lead MCx-B, MCx Tech-A, MCx Tech-B) dedicated to ensuring **every mechanical system is installed correctly, inspected thoroughly, and started up successfully**
   - Double coverage enables **comprehensive pre-startup inspections, detailed startup observations, and rigorous quality verification**

2. **First-Time Quality Focus:**
   - **100% equipment inspections** (no sampling during L2-L3)
   - **Detailed pre-startup verification** to catch installation deficiencies before energization
   - **Comprehensive startup oversight** with vendor representatives to ensure proper commissioning
   - **Immediate issue identification and resolution** during L2-L3, not L4-L5
   - **Thorough documentation** to support seamless transition to functional testing

3. **Risk Mitigation:**
   - **Eliminates "surprises" during L4-L5** that consume schedule contingency
   - **Reduces re-testing requirements** by ensuring systems pass functional tests the first time
   - **Protects the 6-week L4-L5 window** by delivering fully operational, verified systems ready for performance verification
   - **Builds confidence** in system readiness before committing to functional testing

4. **Return on Investment:**
   - **Prevents costly delays** during L4-L5 when time is most critical
   - **Accelerates L4-L5 execution** by eliminating installation-related issues
   - **Reduces vendor call-backs** for startup issues discovered late in commissioning
   - **Ensures schedule compliance** for phase deliveries

**Deployment Timeline:**
- **During Phase 0:** Team B operates with **8-person team** (4 Electrical + 4 Mechanical) for Phases 1-6 L2-L3
- **L4-L5 Rotation:** Team A and Team B maintain balanced 8-person teams throughout all phases

**The Bottom Line:**

By investing in **comprehensive L2-L3 quality assurance**, PGCIS protects Hut 8's aggressive 6-week L4-L5 schedule. **First-time installation quality is non-negotiable** when schedule contingency is minimal. Our dual team approach ensures every system is thoroughly verified and fully operational **before** functional testing begins, maximizing the probability of **first-time test success** and **on-time phase delivery.**

1. **Planning Phase:**
   - Upcoming L4-L5 lead reviews all L2-L3 documentation
   - Technical briefing from L2-L3 team
   - Site walkdown and system familiarization
   - Review of outstanding issues

2. **H2C (Handover to Commissioning) Meeting:**
   - Joint meeting with both teams
   - Formal handoff from L3 to L4
   - Prerequisite review
   - Test script review and finalization

3. **L4-L5 Execution:**
   - Lead team executes testing
   - Support team provides peer review
   - Both teams attend critical tests
   - Daily coordination meetings

4. **Completion and Handoff:**
   - Joint final inspection
   - Documentation review
   - Lessons learned session
   - Transition to operations

### 4.3 Contingency Deployment Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Lead Engineer Absence (Planned)**
- **Trigger:** Vacation, training, scheduled absence
- **Response:**
  - Opposite team lead assumes responsibility
  - Contingent engineer mobilized if extended absence
  - Technicians provide continuity

**Scenario 2: Lead Engineer Absence (Unplanned)**
- **Trigger:** Illness, emergency, unexpected absence
- **Response:**
  - Opposite team lead assumes immediate responsibility
  - Cx Manager assesses duration and mobilizes contingent engineer if needed
  - Work continues without delay

**Scenario 3: Technician Absence**
- **Trigger:** Any absence
- **Response:**
  - Opposite team technician provides coverage
  - Lead engineers assist with critical field work
  - Contingent engineer mobilized for extended absence

**Scenario 4: Concurrent Critical Activities**
- **Trigger:** Multiple phases reaching L4-L5 simultaneously
- **Response:**
  - Both teams fully engaged
  - Contingent engineers mobilized for both disciplines
  - SME support activated for critical tests
  - Schedule adjusted if necessary to maintain quality

**Scenario 5: Cx Manager Absence**
- **Trigger:** Any absence
- **Response:**
  - Designated backup (Lead ECx-A or Lead MCx-A) assumes authority
  - Cx Scheduler provides continuity for coordination
  - Client notification and coordination maintained

**Scenario 6: Schedule Acceleration**
- **Trigger:** Client request for schedule compression
- **Response:**
  - Contingent engineers mobilized to both teams
  - SME support increased frequency
  - Weekend/extended hours coverage implemented
  - Resource surge coordinated through Cx Manager

## 6. QUALITY ASSURANCE AND PEER REVIEW

### 6.1 Peer Review Process

**Design Review:**
- Joint review by both teams during design phase
- Cross-discipline review (electrical reviews mechanical sequences, vice versa)
- Fresh eyes on design commissionability
- Documented comments and resolutions

**Test Script Review:**
- L1-L3 scripts reviewed by opposite team leads
- L4-L5 scripts reviewed by both teams and SMEs
- Technical accuracy verification
- Completeness and safety review

**Testing Observation:**
- Opposite team attends critical L4-L5 tests
- Peer observation and verification
- Independent data collection
- Quality control

**Documentation Review:**
- Cross-team review of commissioning reports
- Document controllers coordinate quality checks
- Cx Manager final review and approval

### 6.2 Quality Control Checkpoints

**Level 1 (Red Tag):**
- Team executing reviews own work
- Doc Control verifies documentation completeness
- Random sampling by opposite team lead (10%)
- Cx Manager spot checks (5%)

**Level 2 (Yellow Tag):**
- Team executing reviews own work
- Doc Control verifies documentation completeness
- Random sampling by opposite team lead (15%)
- Cx Manager spot checks (10%)

**Level 3 (Green Tag):**
- Team executing reviews own work
- Critical equipment: opposite team lead observes
- Doc Control verifies documentation completeness
- Cx Manager reviews all critical equipment

**Level 4 (Blue Tag):**
- Lead team executes and documents
- Support team observes and peer reviews
- Both doc controllers verify documentation
- Cx Manager reviews all test results

**Level 5 (White Tag):**
- Lead team executes and documents
- Support team observes and peer reviews
- Both doc controllers verify documentation
- Cx Manager reviews and approves all IST results
- SMEs review critical aspects

- After each phase completion

**Process Refinement:**
- Regular review of procedures

## 8. RISK MITIGATION STRATEGIES

### 8.1 Identified Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|---------------------|
| **Key personnel absence** | Built-in redundancy with contingent engineers; cross-training between teams |
| **Concurrent critical activities** | Contingent engineers mobilize; SME support activated; schedule optimization |
| **Schedule acceleration** | Flexible resource pool; proven surge capacity; contingent resources on standby |
| **Technical complexity** | SME support layer; peer review process; dual team knowledge base |
| **Communication gaps** | Structured communication protocols; daily huddles; shared platforms |
| **Documentation bottlenecks** | Two document controllers; coordinated workflows; commissioning platform |
| **Quality issues** | Peer review process; QA checkpoints; Cx Manager oversight |
| **Phase transitions** | Formal handoff procedures; joint meetings; documentation reviews |
| **Client changes** | Flexible team structure; scalable resources; rapid response capability |
| **Vendor coordination** | Consistent team presence; established relationships; clear communication |

### 8.2 Escalation Procedures

**Level 1 – Field Issue:**
- Technicians or Lead Engineers resolve
- Documentation in Issue Resolution Log
- No escalation needed

**Level 2 – Technical Issue:**
- Lead Engineers collaborate
- SME consultation if needed
- Cx Manager informed

**Level 3 – Schedule Impact:**
- Lead Engineers assess impact
- Cx Manager and Cx Scheduler develop mitigation
- Client communication
- Contingent resource mobilization if needed

**Level 4 – Program Risk:**
- Cx Manager escalates to Client
- Senior leadership engagement
- Resource surge deployment
- Schedule recovery plan developed

**Level 5 – Safety or Critical Issue:**
- Immediate work stoppage if required
- Cx Manager immediate notification
- Client immediate notification
- Root cause investigation
- Corrective action plan before resumption
