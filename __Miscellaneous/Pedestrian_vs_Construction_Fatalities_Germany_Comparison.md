## Executive Summary

This document compares pedestrian traffic fatalities with construction industry workplace fatalities in Germany over approximately the last 20 years. The analysis reveals that **pedestrian traffic deaths significantly exceed construction workplace deaths**, with pedestrians experiencing 5-8 times more fatalities annually than construction workers.

**Key Finding:** While both categories show declining trends, pedestrians remain at substantially higher risk of fatal incidents than construction workers in Germany.

### Fatality Ratios

| 2020-2023 | 385 | 76 | **5.1:1** |

*Note: 2005-2009 construction data is estimated based on 2011-2021 average

### Key Insights

1. **Magnitude difference:** Pedestrians experience 5-8x more fatalities than construction workers annually

2. **Declining trends:** Both categories show improvement:
   - Pedestrians: 36% reduction (2005-2023)
   - Construction: 26% reduction in accidents (2011-2022)
   - Both: Converging ratio suggests construction safety improvements may be outpacing pedestrian safety

3. **Risk exposure:**
   - **Pedestrians:** General population exposure daily
   - **Construction workers:** Occupational exposure to ~800,000 workers in German construction industry

4. **Every X days statistic:**
   - A pedestrian dies in Germany: **Every 20 hours** (442 deaths ÷ 365 days = 1.2 per day)
   - A construction worker dies in Germany: **Every 4-5 days** (85 deaths ÷ 365 days = 0.23 per day)

## 4. Comparative Context

### Population Exposure

**Pedestrians:**
- Entire German population (84+ million) exposed
- Daily exposure for most citizens
- Urban concentration: 62% of urban traffic deaths

**Construction workers:**
- Approximately 800,000 construction workers in Germany
- Occupational exposure only
- Concentrated risk on job sites

### Fatality Rate Per 100,000 Exposed

**Pedestrians (rough estimate):**
- Deaths per 100,000 population: ~0.5 (442 ÷ 84,000,000 × 100,000)
- Deaths per 100 million km walked: 1.9 (2008-2009 data)

**Construction workers:**
- Deaths per 100,000 workers: ~10.6 (85 ÷ 800,000 × 100,000)
- Approximately **21x higher occupational risk** than pedestrian exposure risk

### Risk Profile Comparison

## 5. Analysis and Observations

### Why Do More Pedestrians Die?

1. **Universal exposure:** Every German citizen is a pedestrian at some point
2. **Volume:** Millions of pedestrian-vehicle interactions daily vs. limited construction workforce
3. **Vulnerable road users:** No protective equipment or structures
4. **Urban concentration:** High population density areas have high pedestrian traffic

### Why Is Construction Risk Higher Per Capita?

1. **High-hazard environment:** Falls, equipment, structural failures
2. **Complex operations:** Multiple simultaneous hazards
3. **Height work:** Significant portion of fatalities from falls
4. **Heavy equipment:** Building vehicles, cranes, loaders

### Policy Implications

**Pedestrian safety:**
- Largest absolute impact potential (442 lives at stake annually)
- Infrastructure interventions needed (crosswalks, traffic calming, speed limits)
- Urban planning focus required
- Recent uptick (2021-2023) warrants attention

**Construction safety:**
- Highest per-capita occupational risk
- Regulatory enforcement critical
- Industry-specific interventions effective
- Continued improvement trend should be maintained

## 6. Data Quality and Limitations

### Pedestrian Data
- **Source:** UNECE Data Portal, German Federal Statistical Office (Destatis)
- **Quality:** High - standardized reporting, 30-day mortality definition
- **Completeness:** Excellent (2005-2023 annual data available)
- **Reliability:** Very high - legal requirement to report all traffic fatalities

### Construction Data
- **Source:** DGUV, IG BAU, BAuA, German Construction Industry Association
- **Quality:** Moderate - multiple reporting systems, underreporting acknowledged
- **Completeness:** Limited (comprehensive data only for 2011-2023)
- **Reliability:** Moderate - "figures are not very reliable" (WSWS report)
- **Limitation:** Not all accidents reported to insurance associations

### Comparison Validity

- Different reporting mechanisms and definitions
- Different exposure populations
- Different risk profiles (universal vs. occupational)
- Construction data less complete for early years (2005-2010)

---

## 7. Conclusions

1. **Absolute numbers:** Pedestrian fatalities (442/year) significantly exceed construction fatalities (78/year) by a factor of 5-6x

2. **Relative risk:** Construction workers face ~21x higher per-capita mortality risk than the general population's pedestrian risk

3. **Trends:** Both categories improving, but pedestrian progress (36% decline) outpaces construction (26% decline in accidents)

4. **Recent concerns:** Pedestrian deaths increased 18% from 2021-2023, reversing decade-long improvements

5. **Policy priority:** Both deserve continued attention:
   - **Pedestrians:** Largest absolute lives saved potential
   - **Construction:** Highest occupational risk requiring sector-specific intervention

6. **Data gaps:** Construction fatality data needs improvement in reporting completeness and consistency
